    Results

    Conclusions

    Questions

    • What level of prompting and therefore required knowledge (DOK-Level) about the subject is sufficient to score better than the avg. Student answers?
    • How close does a good prompting get to the tutor's answer? (What DOK-Level is required to perform a good prompting?)
    • What differences exist based on cognitive processes?
    • Is it possible to overcome the required knowledge (i.e. DOK Level) when solving an exercise using ChatGPT?
    • viability of ChatGPT as a tool for solving educational exercises
    • potential shortcomings and explore required prompts necessary for achieving good outcomes

    Statements

    • Exercises that involve cognitive processes such as the creation or application of knowledge show lower quality responses by chatGPT when used with low to no prior knowledge (avg. 4/10) as opposed to exercises involving cognitive processes such as analysing or evaluating (avg. 6/10). This could be related to the application of untrained concepts or the creation of output based on untrained concepts whereas exercises concerning evaluation and analysis were focused on combining different information (e.g., matching company objectives to project objectives). The model performed better in matching given information as opposed to creating or applying unknown concepts to existing information.
    • Those exercises (cognitive processes creation or application) also had the highest potential improving the quality (avg. 2.6% vs 1.6%) by applying specific knowledge to the prompts.
    • Creating prompts with no to low prior knowledge (DOK-Level 0) already achieved responses of average quality (4.5/10). In most cases, they response was well written and provided a sound reasoning of information provided. Only by looking at the correct application of learned concepts from the course the responses often failed to match the quality of the reference solution.
    • Prompts with medium prior knowledge applied scored slightly better (avg. 5.2/10) than prompts with low to no prior knowledge (avg. 4.2/10). Although setting the context by providing a role (e.g., act as a project manager) had minor to no effect on the quality, the definition of specific concepts (e.g., using SMART-Method or using Influence/Power Grid) increased the quality of the response. Furthermore, providing additional expectations on the formatting of the response increased readability and structure.
    • Incorporating more specialised knowledge about the subject into the prompt such as hints, caveats the AI should consider as well as very precise definitions of expected outcomes that could be obtained by understanding meticulously reading the exercise materials provided increased the quality of the responses a lot. However, the responses often did not meet the tutor's solution as there were still inconsistencies and a lack of accuracy that could not be avoided by engineering the prompts. When dealing on such a high level of base knowledge, the only remaining benefits are related to efficiency gains by pre populating a pretty good and especially well formatted and formalised response that can be further adjusted and improved.
    • When looking at the cognitive complexity required of students to complete a specific exercise task we can say that tasks classified as DOK-Level 2 to DOK-level 4 can be completed (avg. score 4.5/10) by using ChatGPT prompts with low to no prior knowledge. However, due to the lack of knowledge required, it is hard for students to justify the response or even improve it.

    Impact on Learning Process

    Questions

    • ways educators can enhance student learning through effective exercises with the increasing utilisation of AI in the classroom
    • challenges and opportunities associated with ChatGPT's role in solving educational exercises

    Statements

    • By looking at responses of average quality (avg. score 4.5/10) produced by ChatGPT prompts where low to no prior knowledge is required, we can state that students can get along with average scored exercises. However, looking at the result despite the lack of knowledge of concepts and course theory we can assume that students wouldn't acquire any further knowledge using ChatGPT. We can even say that there is a high risk involved that the average responses are incorrect or inaccurate and do not align with expected concepts and or theory from the lecture (Source: ) . This risk would even turn into a disaster thinking of a scenario where students use ChatGPT to learn wrong or false concepts where wasting time would be the least concern.
    • However, when students already know the fundamental theoretical concepts and are training their application on unknown use-cases or practical case studies, ChatGPT can support the learning effect. This is also shown by our prompts where advanced learners try to iteratively reach an accurate and sufficient response based on prompt engineering. By working on the formalisation of chatGPT prompts, reading and challenging it's response and then iteratively reformalise the prompt or by weaving theoretical knowledge into the prompt, the students will interact with ChatGPT and therefore increase their learning experience and depth of understanding. During our research study, we experienced this by using different prompts with different knowledge prerequisites.
    • Especially in a flipped classroom setting it can be beneficial for students to use ChatGPT as a sparring partner and e-tutor to receive different explanations or hands-on guidance when solving exercises on their own (Source: ).
    • But it is of imperative importance to ensure that students possess the required base knowhow in order to understand, challenge and question the responses by ChatGPT. Furthermore, Students should be allowed to experiment with different prompts, comparing the output and discussing the pro's and con's of each response.

    1. Cognitive Process: Cognitive Processes according to Anderson and Krathwohl's revised taxonomy based on Bloom's taxonomy
    2. Prompt Categories: The categories reflect the knowledge pre requisites that are required by students to write the prompt.
      • Low: Assumption there is low to no knowledge available and there will be primarily a copy & paste job of the exercise description.
      • Medium: Assumption there is medium knowledge about the subject available to write the prompt. This could be:
        • GenAI Prompting Know-How: Telling the AI to act in a specific role (e.g. Project Manager) and only include a relevant subset of the provided exercise use-case.
        • Subject Knowhow: Providing used key terminologies from the lecture
      • High: Assumption there is strong knowledge about the subject available to write the prompt: This could be:
        • GenAI Prompting Know-How: Telling the AI to act in a specific role (e.g. Project Manager) and only include a relevant subset of the provided exercise use-case. Furthermore, specify more concretely what to expect in the response.
        • Subject Knowhow: Making a concrete example of a good response that should be replicated and adopted by the GenAI. Use specific terminologies from the lecture and provide hints to create a good response.

    Variables:

    • Independent Variable: Base know-how level (Low, Medium, High). Prompts will be flagged accordingly.
    • Dependent Variables: Accuracy and usefulness of generative AI responses (rating 1 - 10 in comparison against tutor's solution). Responses will be rated accordingly.

    Usage of GenAI-Model: ChatGPT 3.5
